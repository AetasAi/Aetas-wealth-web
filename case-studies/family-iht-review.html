Case Study

Helping a family review their inheritance tax exposure

All client details have been anonymised. Names and identifying information have been changed to protect client confidentiality.

Adviser Peter Rose APFS, Chartered Financial Planner & Pensions Specialist
Area Inheritance tax planning, estate planning, pension IHT

Situation

A couple in their late sixties came to Aetas Wealth having been referred by their solicitor. Both were retired professionals. They had three adult children and four grandchildren. Their estate consisted of the family home, investment portfolios, ISAs, cash savings and two defined contribution pension pots that had not been drawn on.

They had Wills in place, but these had been written over fifteen years earlier. They had not reviewed their estate since. Neither had considered what the combined estate might look like from an inheritance tax perspective, and neither had factored in the Finance Act 2026 pension changes.

Challenge

When Aetas Wealth mapped the combined estate, the position was clear: both the nil-rate band and residence nil-rate band would be exhausted, with substantial taxable value remaining. The pension pots — previously outside the estate for IHT purposes — would come within scope from April 2027 under the Finance Act 2026, which added further to the taxable estate.

The couple's immediate concern was that they did not want a significant portion of what they had built to pass to HMRC rather than to their children and grandchildren. But they also had no desire to give away more than they were comfortable with, and needed to ensure their own income and security came first.

Approach

The work began with a full estate map: all assets, current valuations, existing Will arrangements and the interaction between pension death benefits and the estate. Cash flow modelling established how much the couple needed to retain for their own income security through retirement — making clear what was genuinely surplus to their needs and therefore available for planning.

From that foundation, the work covered four areas. A structured annual gifting programme was established, using the annual gift exemptions for both the children and grandchildren, with amounts calibrated to the cash flow model so the couple could give with confidence. Second, the existing Wills were flagged for urgent review with the solicitor, particularly in relation to the spousal exemption and the residence nil-rate band. Third, the role of the pension pots was reviewed: with the April 2027 changes approaching, Peter worked through the options for drawdown sequencing — whether to draw on the pensions sooner, use other assets first, or take other steps to reduce the taxable pension value over time. Finally, a modest investment into AIM-listed portfolios was considered as part of a Business Relief strategy for a portion of the surplus investment portfolio.

Outcome

The couple left with a clear picture of their estate for the first time. They had a gifting programme in place, their Wills had been updated by the solicitor, their pension planning had been restructured in light of the April 2027 changes, and they had a written financial plan covering their own income needs and the estate strategy together.

They described the greatest value as simply knowing: knowing what the exposure was, knowing what they could do about it, and knowing that their plan was consistent with their own financial security.

Case Study

Helping clients approaching 75 understand their pension options

All client details have been anonymised. Names and identifying information have been changed to protect client confidentiality.

Adviser Peter Rose APFS, Chartered Financial Planner & Pensions Specialist
Area Pension planning, later life planning, pension IHT

Situation

The client was a widow in her mid-seventies who had been managing a flexi-access drawdown arrangement from her late husband's pension alongside her own defined contribution pension, which she had not yet touched. She had an adult son and daughter, both financially independent. She lived comfortably and her income from the State Pension, a small defined benefit pension and modest drawdown withdrawals was sufficient for her needs.

She came to Aetas Wealth after hearing about the Finance Act 2026 pension changes through her son. He had read that pensions would become subject to inheritance tax from April 2027 and was concerned about what this meant for his mother's position. She was unclear about what the age 75 pension review involved and whether she needed to take any action.

Challenge

The client had two pension pots: the inherited drawdown arrangement and her own untouched defined contribution pension. Together, these formed a substantial part of her estate. Under the pre-2027 rules, both would have passed outside of her estate for IHT purposes. Under the Finance Act 2026, both would be brought within scope from April 2027.

The age 75 milestone itself brings specific considerations around pension tax treatment — in particular, how benefits are tested against lifetime allowance-related rules and the tax treatment of death benefits. The client needed clarity on what decisions, if any, were triggered by approaching 75, and how those decisions interacted with the April 2027 changes.

Approach

Peter Rose began by separating the two questions clearly for the client. The age 75 review and the Finance Act 2026 changes are distinct matters, but they interact in her situation, and understanding both was the starting point.

A full review of both pension arrangements was carried out: current fund values, existing death benefit nominations, how the inherited drawdown was structured, and the tax treatment of each pot on death under both the existing rules and the rules taking effect from April 2027.

Cash flow modelling was run to establish whether the client needed to draw more from the pensions to meet her income needs, or whether — as was the case — she was currently drawing less than she needed to from a tax efficiency perspective. This is a common position for clients in their seventies: pension funds continue to grow untouched while tax-efficient withdrawal opportunities are missed.

From this foundation, Peter worked through three areas with the client. First, an adjusted drawdown strategy: rather than taking only what was needed for day-to-day income, a more considered level of annual withdrawal was established — sufficient to make use of her income tax personal allowance fully, and to gradually reduce the taxable pension estate ahead of April 2027. Second, death benefit nominations were reviewed and updated for both pensions to ensure they reflected her current wishes and family position. Third, the interaction with her wider estate was considered: how the pensions sat alongside her other assets, what her overall IHT exposure looked like, and whether any further action on the non-pension estate was appropriate.

Outcome

The client left with clarity on a situation that had felt confusing and concerning. Her drawdown strategy was adjusted to make better use of her annual tax-free allowances. Her death benefit nominations were updated. She had a clear view of her overall estate position and what the April 2027 changes would mean for her family.

Her son attended the review meeting with her. He described the process as enormously reassuring — not because the tax position had disappeared, but because his mother now had a plan and understood it.

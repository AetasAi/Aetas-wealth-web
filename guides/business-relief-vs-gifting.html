Business Relief vs gifting for inheritance tax

Two commonly used tools for managing IHT exposure — and how they differ.

Short answer: Gifting transfers assets out of the estate — the donor no longer owns them. Business Relief allows certain qualifying assets to remain in the estate and be exempt from IHT, without giving them away. Both have significant conditions and limitations.

Gifting

An outright gift removes an asset from your estate. If you survive for seven years after making the gift, it falls outside your estate entirely for IHT purposes. Gifts in the seven years before death are potentially chargeable, with tapering relief applying from year three onwards.

Annual gift exemptions allow smaller gifts each year to fall outside IHT immediately: up to £3,000 per year (plus any unused allowance from the previous year), plus exemptions for gifts on marriage and for regular gifts made from surplus income.

The practical constraint with gifting is obvious: once you give an asset away, it belongs to the recipient. This may be exactly what is intended — for example, giving a lump sum to an adult child — but it requires the donor to be comfortable relinquishing both the asset and control over how it is used.

Business Relief

Business Relief (BR) exempts qualifying business assets from inheritance tax — without requiring them to be given away. The asset remains in the estate; it is simply exempt from the tax charge.

To qualify for 100% BR, the asset must be a qualifying business interest or shares in a qualifying company. In the investment context, the most commonly used route is investment in AIM-listed companies that qualify as trading businesses, typically through a managed portfolio. The shares must have been held for at least two years.

The key advantage is that the investor retains the asset. If they need to access capital, they can sell. If they need income, they can draw it. There is no seven-year clock to run.

The key risk is that AIM investments carry significantly higher investment risk than mainstream equities. AIM companies are smaller, less liquid and more volatile. The qualifying status of individual companies can also change. BR investment portfolios are not appropriate for all investors.

Can they work together?

Yes. A common approach is to use a structured gifting programme for amounts that the client is comfortable transferring permanently, while using BR investment for a portion of the portfolio where the client wants to retain access to capital. The two approaches are complementary rather than mutually exclusive.

Considerations

  • BR investment in AIM companies carries significantly higher risk than mainstream investing and is not suitable for all clients.
  • Gifting requires a genuine transfer of ownership — arrangements designed to retain the benefit of a gift may not be effective for IHT.
  • Cash flow modelling is important before any gifting programme: the client must retain enough for their own needs.
  • The qualifying status of BR investments can change — ongoing professional review is important.

When professional advice may help

Both gifting and Business Relief involve complex rules and interactions with other taxes. Professional advice ensures that any planning is structured correctly, consistent with the client's broader financial position and reviewed as circumstances change.

Financial adviser vs wealth manager — what is the difference?

A plain-English guide to two terms that are often confused.

Short answer: A financial adviser provides regulated advice across a broad range of financial planning areas. A wealth manager typically focuses on investment management. The terms overlap, and some firms do both — but the scope and regulatory context differ.

What is a financial adviser?

A financial adviser is a regulated professional who advises clients on financial matters. In the UK, this advice must be provided under FCA authorisation — either directly or as an appointed representative of an authorised firm. The advice can cover a wide range of areas: pensions, retirement planning, investments, protection (life cover, income protection, critical illness), inheritance tax planning, estate planning and cash flow modelling.

A financial adviser's role is to understand a client's overall financial position and objectives, and to provide recommendations that are in the client's best interest. This is a regulated activity: advisers must be qualified, adhere to the FCA's Conduct of Business rules, and operate within a framework designed to protect clients.

What is a wealth manager?

Wealth management is a term used more loosely. It typically describes services that combine investment management with some level of broader financial planning, often aimed at clients with higher levels of investable assets. Some wealth managers are also full financial advisers. Others are primarily investment managers who provide limited advice outside the investment portfolio itself.

The term is not regulated in the same way that "financial adviser" implies a regulated activity. Anyone can call themselves a wealth manager, but investment management and financial advice are regulated activities and must be conducted under FCA authorisation.

What does independent mean?

In the UK regulated advice market, advisers are classified as either independent or restricted. An independent financial adviser can consider products and solutions from across the whole market — they are not limited to any particular provider or panel. A restricted adviser can only recommend from a defined range of products or providers.

Independent advice is considered the broader standard and is generally preferred by clients who want whole-of-market recommendations. Aetas Wealth provides independent financial advice.

Key differences at a glance

Regulated activity Yes — FCA authorisation required Investment management is regulated; the term itself is not
Scope Broad: pensions, retirement, IHT, protection, investments, estate planning Typically investment-focused, sometimes broader
Independence Can be independent (whole of market) or restricted Varies widely by firm
Client profile Individuals, families, business owners — wide range Often high-net-worth, investable-asset-focused
Planning approach Whole-client financial plan across all areas Investment portfolio as primary focus

Considerations

  • If your primary need is investment management and your affairs are relatively straightforward, a specialist investment manager may be appropriate.
  • If your needs span pensions, retirement, inheritance tax, protection and estate planning, a financial adviser providing a whole-of-client plan is likely to be more appropriate.
  • The two are not mutually exclusive: many financial advisers also manage investments as part of a broader financial plan.
  • Always check that any firm or individual you engage is authorised by the FCA. The FCA register at register.fca.org.uk confirms authorisation status.
  • Ask whether advice is independent or restricted — this affects the range of products and solutions an adviser can recommend.

When professional advice may help

Independent financial advice is worth considering when your financial position is complex enough that decisions in one area affect others — for example, when pension planning interacts with inheritance tax, or when business exit proceeds need to be structured alongside income in retirement. The value of holistic advice is that it considers all areas together rather than in isolation.

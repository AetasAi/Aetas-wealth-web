Inheritance tax planning options explained

A plain-English guide to the main tools available for reducing IHT exposure.

Short answer: The main options for managing inheritance tax exposure are: using nil-rate bands and exemptions, gifting, trusts, Business Relief, Agricultural Property Relief and — until April 2027 — pension planning. Each has its own rules, conditions and limitations. A structured plan typically combines several approaches.

The nil-rate band and residence nil-rate band

Everyone has a nil-rate band (NRB) — currently £325,000 — below which no inheritance tax is charged. Any unused NRB from a deceased spouse or civil partner can be transferred to the surviving partner, giving a combined threshold of up to £650,000.

The residence nil-rate band (RNRB) provides an additional allowance of up to £175,000 per person where a main residence is left to direct descendants. Combined with the NRB, a couple leaving their home to their children can effectively shield up to £1 million from IHT — though the RNRB tapers away for estates above £2 million.

Both thresholds have been frozen until April 2028. With house prices and investment values rising, more estates are falling into the IHT net each year.

Gifting

Outright gifts made more than seven years before death are generally exempt from IHT — these are known as potentially exempt transfers (PETs). Gifts made in the seven years before death may still be subject to IHT, with the rate tapering down the further back the gift was made.

There are also annual gift exemptions that fall outside this seven-year rule: each person can give away up to £3,000 per year free of IHT (plus unused allowance from the previous year), and there are exemptions for gifts on marriage and for regular gifts from surplus income.

A structured gifting programme — calibrated carefully against cash flow modelling to ensure that income and capital needs are maintained — can reduce the taxable estate meaningfully over time.

Trusts

Assets placed into certain types of trust can be removed from the taxable estate, subject to conditions and potential immediate IHT charges on the transfer. Trusts can be used to pass assets to the next generation while retaining some control over how and when they are used.

Trust planning is complex and the tax treatment depends on the type of trust, the assets involved and the circumstances of the settlor. Will trusts, discretionary trusts and bare trusts each operate differently. Professional legal and financial advice is important before establishing a trust for IHT purposes.

Business Relief

Business Relief (BR) provides relief from inheritance tax on qualifying business assets. Assets qualifying for 100% BR — including interests in unlisted trading companies and, in many cases, shares in AIM-listed companies — are completely exempt from IHT, provided they have been held for at least two years at the time of death.

BR is a significant planning tool, particularly for business owners and for investors seeking IHT-efficient investment options. Some investment managers offer AIM-focused portfolios specifically designed to qualify for BR. However, AIM investments carry high risk and the qualifying status of any individual investment can change.

Agricultural Property Relief

Agricultural Property Relief (APR) operates similarly to Business Relief for qualifying agricultural land and property. It is relevant primarily for farming families and those with interests in agricultural land.

Pensions — and the Finance Act 2026 change

Until April 2027, unspent pension funds sit outside the estate for inheritance tax purposes. This made pensions a powerful estate planning tool: by drawing income from other assets first and leaving the pension intact, the pension pot could pass to beneficiaries free of IHT.

From 6 April 2027, this changes. The Finance Act 2026 brings unspent pension funds within the scope of inheritance tax for the first time. Pension pots in drawdown, uncrystallised funds and death benefits will now form part of the taxable estate. The implications are significant — particularly for clients who have specifically structured their drawdown strategy around the pension's IHT-free status.

A review of pension strategy in light of the April 2027 changes is one of the most time-sensitive planning actions available to clients before the change takes effect. Further detail on the April 2027 pension IHT changes is available here.

Considerations

  • IHT planning should always preserve sufficient assets and income for the client's own needs — giving too much away too early can create dependency risk.
  • Gifting is simple and effective but the seven-year rule means it requires time to become fully effective.
  • Business Relief investments carry risk — qualifying status is not guaranteed, and AIM shares can be volatile.
  • Trust structures are effective but complex and require proper legal documentation.
  • The pension position has changed materially — anyone with a pension above a modest size should review their estate plan.
  • A good IHT plan typically combines several of these tools, calibrated to the client's assets, income needs, family circumstances and timeline.

When professional advice may help

Inheritance tax planning involves interactions between several areas of law and finance: income tax, capital gains tax, trust law, Will drafting and pension rules. An effective plan requires these to be considered together. A financial adviser working alongside a private client solicitor is typically the most effective combination.
